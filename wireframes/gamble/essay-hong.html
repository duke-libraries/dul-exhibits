Photographing Encounters, Archives Reanimated

Through his lens, Sidney D. Gamble witnessed China during the turbulent years of the first half of the twentieth century. His camera recorded significant historical events, such as the 1918 World War I victory celebration in the Forbidden City, the May 4th movement in 1919, and Dr. Sun Yat-sen’s funeral in 1925. However, as a trained sociologist equipped with Western technology, Gamble was also a keen observer of the modernization of China. His camera captured the unfolding of institutional changes (such as the emergence of modern schools, prisons, and hospitals), the arrival of new modes of transportation, and the expressions of people in all stages of life and from all strata of society. Gamble was at once a documentarian, a sociologist, and more.

Figure 1

Gamble looked and people looked back. His photography shows an open encounter between him, a four-time sojourner in China, and the people he met. We see faces that were aware and curious, bodies that were mobile and even defiant. In many of Gamble’s photographs, he afforded the subjects of his gaze the agency to return that gaze. It is no accident or coincidence that in many photographs we clearly see the shadow of the photographer in the frame. While not necessarily intentionally self-reflexive, they are physical traces of the co-existence of the photographer and his subjects in place and time. Gamble himself evidently did not regard these shadows as technical or aesthetic flaws—many of them appear in hand-colored lantern slides intended for public lectures. These stunning images of encounter are history awaiting reanimation, an invitation for re-encountering Gamble’s China today.
