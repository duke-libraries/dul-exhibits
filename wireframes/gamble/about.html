About the Sidney D. Gamble Photograph Collection at Duke Uniersity Libraries

Sidney Gamble took his first images of China in 1908 during a trip with his family. In 1917, he joined the work of the Princeton University Center in China and became a secretary for the Beijing YMCA. The following year, while teaching elementary economics at Yenching University, he was invited to conduct a social survey of Beijing’s population. His research resulted in Peking: A Social Survey, the first publication of its kind, with rich data on the occupations, income, health and recreation of Beijing’s population.

Figure 1

Gamble’s work as a sociologist gave him access to wide-ranging elements of Chinese society, a level of intimacy that in turn fed his interest as an amateur photographer. Between 1917 and 1932, Gamble would travel to China three times to collect data for socio-economic surveys. Each time he would capture images of urban and rural life, public events, architecture, religious statuary, and the countryside.

Figure 2

About fifteen years after Gamble’s death, his daughter, Catherine Curran, found a trove of nitrate negatives in a closet in the family’s home in New York. Stored in beautiful rosewood boxes, the negatives were housed in individual paper sleeves, annotated with typed and handwritten captions. In 2006, Duke University and Ms. Curran reached an agreement to place Gamble’s photographs in the David M. Rubenstein Rare Book & Manuscript Library. The process of digitizing the highly flammable nitrate negatives started immediately, and Gamble’s typed and handwritten labels were transcribed to provide captions and geographic headings for the digitized images.

The complete collection of digitized photographs was published in the fall of 2008 on the Duke University Libraries website, marking the first time that this large body of photographs of China, Korea, Japan, Hawaii, San Francisco, and Russia has been available to the public.
