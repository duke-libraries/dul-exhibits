Penetrating the Past

Sidney Gamble's photographs, like all photographs, record an eventâ€”the encounter between the photographer and the photographed. Understood this way, his images of China are more than a document of changing ways of life, but are a record of the photographic encounter itself. How does Gamble's presence make itself known in many of these images? How does this sometimes happen in ways beyond his intent or control?

Figure 1

In an image titled "Buying Eggs," for example, Gamble captures a daily scene in a Beijing market: a late-middle-aged woman sits behind a basket of eggs, dressed in warm clothes in spite of the sun shining directly on her, while a younger and better-dressed woman crouches down to inspect the seller's wares. The same sunlight on the bundled vendor's face shines also on Gamble's back, casting his shadow into the lower part of the image. In another image, "Loading Baggage Cot Five," two boys break from their work and look directly at the camera. Their gaze meets ours. This startling moment reveals the photographer's presence and raises many questions: how did Gamble get on that windy, mountain road? Whose belongings are being loaded onto that baggage cot, and why is the loading happening mid-journey?

Figure 2

Details like these reveal the photographer's presence, once hidden behind the lens. By raising questions in a viewer's mind, these historical images reanimate the encounter between photographer and photographed, through which the viewer in present time may also be transformed.
