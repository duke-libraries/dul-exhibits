Recalcitrant Flesh: Photographing Women's Bodies

Critical engagement with gender issues sheds a different light on the Gamble Collection. We find instances of women refusing to cooperate with the photographer, for example, in the images of two courtly women walking arm-in-arm, passing by and turning away from Gamble. As Gamble hurries to capture their fascinating costumes, the women's turned backs make a mockery of his eagerness. The photographer's shadow in the foreground dramatizes the relative positioning between the subject and the object in this interaction. Reaching after the two women as they walk away, the presence of Gamble's shadow is reminiscent of the stereotypical, impolite tourist who takes desperate shots of the exotic animal at the zoo as it strolls away with boredom and disdain.

Figure 1: Manchu Woman Front; Manchu Woman Back

While Gamble succeeds in capturing the women's image, the flesh retains its ability to refuse incorporation into historical narratives. In the act of turning away, the female couple forms a homosocial intimacy that is inaccessible to the masculine gaze. In the corporeal refusal to be reduced to mere social positions, the flesh offers a potential for deep-rooted agency. In contrast to film and its embrace of linear time, the photograph is better positioned to jump out of time, carrying female flesh above the historical timeline in a sudden line of flight.

Figure 2: Thanksgiving Day Presidential Review, Manchu Women — Front; Manchu Women — Back

Women's oppression and liberation are key tropes in politicized narratives of feudalism, modernity, and socialism in China. Instead of interpreting women's bodies as markers of the tides of change, we see in this simple turning away from the inquiring, external gaze those women's refusal to pose for the camera; a refusal of reading on our part, then, that constitutes an affirmation of recalcitrant female flesh stripped free of the imposed burden of historical progress.
